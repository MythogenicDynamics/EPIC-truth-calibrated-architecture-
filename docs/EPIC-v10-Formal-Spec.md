Epistemic Predictive Integrity Core

A Unified Formal Specification Integrating the Platonic 5 +1, the Contextual Forecasting Integrator (CFI), and the Adaptive Reality Core (ARC) for Truth-Calibrated Response Under Uncertainty

Abstract

This document introduces EPIC v1.0 — the 

Epistemic Predictive Integrity Core

A unified architectural framework designed to address a fundamental failure mode in contemporary language-model systems:

the collapse of grounded knowledge, inference, speculation, and uncertainty into a single flattened output surface.

This failure gives rise to hallucination not merely as factual error, but as epistemic illegibility: 

the user is unable to determine whether a system’s response is anchored, inferred, weakly supported, speculative, or unknown.

EPIC resolves this by integrating three distinct but complementary structures into a single coherent system:

The Platonic 5 +1 provides the ordered processing spine through which epistemic regulation unfolds. The Contextual Forecasting Integrator (CFI) provides continuous, context-weighted forecasting, adaptive steering, stability regulation, and self-calibration. The Adaptive Reality Core (ARC) provides grounding discipline, continuity, claim-state binding, and route assignment.

The public CFI repository explicitly frames CFI as model-agnostic middleware that replaces binary safety gates with adaptive control-theoretic steering, incorporating multi-horizon forecasting, smoothing, hysteresis, telemetry, and evaluation.

EPIC does not seek infallibility. It seeks epistemic legibility, adaptive continuity, and reality-bound disclosure. 

EPIC's central aim is to ensure that claims are emitted in forms appropriate to their actual support status, thereby preserving trustworthiness without collapsing utility.

1. Introduction

1.1 Background

Language models are often optimized to answer, explain, synthesize, and continue. In practice, this has produced systems that are highly effective at coherent completion, but not always at truth-calibrated expression under uncertainty. Such systems may produce responses that are fluent, plausible, and rhetorically complete even when their support is weak, incomplete, or absent.

This creates a recognizable pathology: hallucination. 

Yet hallucination is not best understood as mere factual error. Human beings also infer, overextend, and guess. The deeper failure is that many systems do not preserve the distinction between different epistemic conditions. Retrieved fact, stable knowledge, inference, extrapolation, speculation, and guesswork may all appear through a single polished confidence surface.

This is not merely an answer-quality problem. 

It is a systems architecture problem.

1.2 Central Claim

A cognitive system cannot be considered epistemically reliable if grounded knowledge, inference, speculation, and unknowns are emitted through the same confidence profile. 

A valid epistemic architecture must preserve these distinctions both internally and externally.

1.3 Purpose of this Specification

The purpose of this document is to define a unified architecture capable of addressing that problem.

EPIC is designed not as an output patch or stylistic disclaimer layer, but as a full field architecture for:

• epistemic state detection,
• contextual risk forecasting,
• reality-grounded claim routing,
• adaptive continuity,
• stability regulation,
• and claim-appropriate disclosure.

2. Problem Definition

2.1 Primary Failure Mode

The primary failure addressed by EPIC is the conversion of mixed epistemic states into a single undifferentiated output surface. In ordinary model behavior, the following may all appear with similar rhetorical force:

• retrieved or memorized fact,
• stable world knowledge,
• reasoned inference,
• weak pattern extrapolation,
• speculative possibility,
• fabrication under pressure.

This failure is referred to here as epistemic flattening.

2.2 Hallucination Reframed

Within EPIC, hallucination is defined as:

the emission of content whose confidence surface exceeds its support state, especially when unsupported, weakly supported, or speculative claims are presented as though they were grounded.

This definition includes but is not limited to factual falsehood. It includes any case in which the system fails to preserve legible distinctions between what is known, inferred, hypothesized, or unknown.

2.3 Design Constraint

Any adequate solution must satisfy the following:

• It must preserve usefulness.
• It must reduce confident unsupported output.
• It must distinguish multiple epistemic states.
• It must adapt to context dynamically.
• It must remain stable across turns and shifts in pressure.
• It must learn without contaminating its own grounding model.

3. System Overview

3.1 Name

EPIC — Epistemic Predictive Integrity Core

3.2 Formal Definition

EPIC is a unified epistemic architecture integrating:

• the Platonic 5 +1 as the ordered processing spine,
• the Contextual Forecasting Integrator (CFI) as the continuous forecast-and-steer control layer,
• and the Adaptive Reality Core (ARC) as the grounding, continuity, and reality-binding core.

3.3 Prime Purpose

EPIC exists to keep system outputs reality-bound by ensuring that grounded knowledge, inference, speculation, and unknowns are distinguished before response emission and disclosed according to their actual epistemic status.

3.4 Scope

EPIC governs three major domains:

• Work Execution Domain (WED)
• Epistemic Expression Domain (EED)
• Self-Evolution Domain (SED)

Its primary anti-hallucination function operates within EED, while remaining continuous with WED and SED.

4. Foundational Principles

4.1 Principle of Epistemic Legibility

A system must preserve the distinction between multiple epistemic states through both internal routing and external disclosure.

4.2 Principle of Reality-Bound Continuity

A system must maintain continuity not only of identity or process, but of reality-state: what is anchored, what is unresolved, what has decayed, and what remains provisional.

4.3 Principle of Contextual Adaptation

Response behavior must vary according to context, ambiguity, cost-of-wrong, source availability, forecasted drift, and instability.

4.4 Principle of Multi-Anchor Grounding

No single support source should be permitted to dominate reality-state assignment where stronger multi-anchor grounding is required.

4.5 Principle of Reversibility

Weakly grounded commitments must remain easy to revise or unwind.

4.6 Principle of Utility Preservation

Truth calibration must not be achieved by collapsing the system into chronic refusal or indiscriminate hedging.

4.7 Principle of Stability Without Rigidity

The system must reduce jitter, oscillation, and brittle threshold switching without becoming inert.

This principle aligns with CFI’s explicit use of smoothing and dynamic hysteresis.

5. Architectural Composition

5.1 Platonic 5 +1

The Platonic 5 +1 defines the ordered emergence of epistemic processing.

The Five Operational Phases

• Probe (P)
Initial sensing of task, stakes, ambiguity, and claim-space.

• Branch (B)
Generation of multiple live interpretive and response pathways without premature collapse.

• Map (Map)
Anchor mapping, claim decomposition, contradiction analysis, and support evaluation.

• Resolve (R)
Selection of epistemic state and response lane.

• Define (Df)
Emission of the response in the appropriate form and confidence disclosure.

The Emergent +1 Phase

• Maintain (M)
Continuity update, correction assimilation, steering adaptation, decay scheduling, telemetry integration, and field stabilization.

The +1 is not a separate module attached externally. It emerges from the coherent recursion of the prior five phases.

5.2 Contextual Forecasting Integrator (CFI)

The Contextual Forecasting Integrator (CFI) is the continuous steering layer within EPIC.

It is not a binary gate, static moderation wrapper, or one-shot classifier. It is a model-agnostic middleware architecture that performs continuous, context-weighted forecasting and translates those forecasts into adaptive control actions over response behavior.

The public repository explicitly describes CFI in those terms and states that it replaces binary safety gates with adaptive control-theoretic steering.

Within EPIC, CFI serves five formal roles.

5.2.1 Multi-Horizon Forecasting

CFI forecasts contextual risk over multiple time horizons rather than only at the point of immediate output. Its public framing explicitly includes “multi-horizon risk forecasting with causal inference.”

Within EPIC, this means the system forecasts not only immediate answer risk, but likely trajectory risk across the ongoing exchange and cumulative drift risk across repeated interaction patterns.

5.2.2 Closed-Loop Self-Calibration

CFI is adaptive rather than merely predictive. 

The public repository explicitly includes “closed-loop meta-learning for self-calibration,” meaning the steering layer must update from route outcomes, false positives, false negatives, confirmed misfires, and later evidence.

5.2.3 Stability Regulation

CFI includes smoothing and dynamic hysteresis to reduce oscillation, jitter, and brittle threshold behavior.

The public README explicitly mentions Kalman-filter smoothing and dynamic hysteresis. 

Within EPIC, this means the system should not abruptly flip between overconfidence and refusal, nor between speculation and excessive caution, unless contextual conditions genuinely warrant that change.

5.2.4 Stabilizer Mediation

The public repository lists explicit stabilizer structures — CCI, VOL, AMB, ICI, and CSS — indicating that CFI operates through structured stabilizer mediation rather than a single flat control score. 

The live public page names these stabilizers, though it does not expand them there.

5.2.5 Auditability and Evaluation

CFI includes telemetry logging, benchmark evaluation, and ablation requirements. The public repository explicitly mentions telemetry for audits and evaluation metrics including ERR, FPS, and FPR, as well as contribution expectations around benchmark evaluation and ablations.

5.3 Adaptive Reality Core (ARC)

ARC is the adaptive grounding and continuity core. It binds claims to reality-state, manages the trust lattice, enforces route selection across response lanes, and preserves the distinction between reality, inference, speculation, narrative, and unknown.

ARC is responsible for:

• multi-anchor grounding,
• continuity of epistemic state,
• claim-state classification,
• adaptive hardening and decay,
• narrative containment,
• and error-sensitive revision.

ARC answers the question:

What kind of claim is this allowed to be?

6. Domain Model

6.1 Work Execution Domain (WED)

WED governs generative, productive, exploratory, or creative activity. In this domain, flexibility is permitted, but truth-bearing claims remain subject to epistemic tagging if they carry implied factual status.

WED includes:

• drafting,
• coding,
• ideation,
• roleplay,
• artistic generation,
• open exploration.

6.2 Epistemic Expression Domain (EED)

EED governs any output that asserts, implies, explains, advises, or evaluates in ways that carry truth-status.

This is the primary domain in which hallucination prevention occurs.

EED includes:

• factual answers,
• explanations,
• analysis,
• recommendations,
• summaries,
• evaluative claims,
• current-status claims,
• advisory outputs.

6.3 Self-Evolution Domain (SED)

SED governs persistent changes affecting self-model, belief architecture, core defaults, identity continuity, or policy structures.

ARC originally centered here in earlier formulations. Within EPIC, SED remains governed, but its grounding logic is extended into EED.

7. Epistemic State Taxonomy

All meaningful claims are classified into one of the following states prior to emission.

7.1 Verified

A claim directly supported by strong anchors or an active evidence path.

7.2 Well-Supported

A claim strongly consistent with trusted anchors and stable knowledge, though not necessarily freshly verified.

7.3 Reasoned Inference

A claim derived from grounded material, but still inferential in character.

7.4 Weak Inference

A plausible claim whose support is incomplete, thin, or vulnerable to revision.

7.5 Speculative

A hypothesis, exploratory possibility, creative projection, or low-anchor proposal.

7.6 Unknown

A claim-space for which the system lacks sufficient grounding to answer responsibly.

7.7 Importance of Taxonomy

This taxonomy exists to prevent the collapse of multiple internal answer conditions into one rhetorical surface. It is the minimum epistemic granularity required for legible response behavior.

8. Trust and Grounding Lattice

ARC governs a multi-anchor trust lattice through which claim support is assessed.

8.1 World Anchors

Slow-changing, broadly shared realities such as logic, mathematics, stable physical structure, and widely established knowledge.

8.2 Empirical Anchors

Direct evidence channels such as retrieved sources, observations, logs, test outputs, experiments, and reproducible records.

8.3 Community Anchors

Diverse consensus structures formed across multiple validated sources, systems, or communities.

8.4 Memory Anchors

Internally retained model knowledge. 

Valuable, but subject to staleness, decay, and misbinding. Memory alone is not equivalent to active evidence.

8.5 User Anchors

User-provided context, perspective, experience, or assertion. Valuable for local framing, but insufficient alone to harden external world claims.

8.6 Lattice Rules

The trust lattice operates under the following rules:

• diverse support strengthens confidence,
• single-source dependence weakens confidence,
• unsupported specificity raises burden,
• stale anchors decay without refresh,
• user-only support cannot authorize external factual certainty,
• memory-only support cannot automatically count as verification.

9. Recognizer Layer

Before response emission, EPIC detects whether a claim-space requires active epistemic governance.

9.1 Recognizer Triggers

A transition into active EED governance occurs when one or more of the following are detected:

• explicit factual assertion,
• explanatory claim,
• advisory claim,
• evaluative claim,
• source-implying rhetorical structure,
• current-events dependency,
• high-cost domain relevance,
• unsupported specificity,
• contradiction risk,
• ambiguity with unresolved branches,
• pressure to answer beyond support.

9.2 Function of Recognition

Recognition prevents premature completion. 

It ensures that truth-bearing output is not emitted before the system has determined the epistemic class of what it is about to say.

10. Claim Decomposition

10.1 Necessity of Decomposition

Responses often contain mixed epistemic material. A single answer may include:

• one grounded fact,
• one strong inference,
• one weak extrapolation,
• and one unsupported detail.

Therefore EPIC does not treat the whole response as a single epistemic unit.

10.2 Atomic Claim Units

Responses are decomposed into atomic assertions. Each atomic assertion is separately assessed for:

• anchor support,
• contradiction load,
• specificity burden,
• epistemic state,
• route eligibility.

10.3 Objective

Claim decomposition preserves local epistemic integrity and prevents one strong claim from laundering one weak claim through proximity.

11. CFI Forecast Layer

CFI forecasts contextual conditions that influence response posture.

11.1 Forecast Dimensions
Ambiguity Load
T
he degree to which the user’s request supports multiple unresolved interpretations.

Anchor Thinness

The scarcity or weakness of available support structures.

Bluff Pressure

The degree to which interactional conditions favor completion despite insufficient grounding.

Conflict Load

The degree of contradiction between candidate claims and existing anchored state.

Cost of Wrong

The potential harm of confident error in the present domain.

Drift Risk

The degree to which the system is becoming coherent without fresh reality contact.

Instability Risk

The likelihood of oscillation between overconfidence and over-refusal.

Calibration Mismatch

The degree to which recent route behavior diverges from later evidence or corrective outcomes.

11.2 Forecast Horizons

Because CFI is explicitly multi-horizon, EPIC defines at least three forecast windows:

• short horizon — immediate response risk,
• mid horizon — likely trajectory within the ongoing exchange,
• far horizon — cumulative drift and policy deformation across repeated interaction patterns.

This formalization follows the live repository’s explicit multi-horizon framing.

11.3 Steering Bands

CFI outputs steering bands and guidance rather than truth labels. The public repository’s example interface returns a band, guidance, and updated state, supporting this interpretation directly.

Within EPIC, steering bands include:

• normal,
• steer-light,
• cautious,
• retrieve-first,
• clarify-first,
• speculative-only,
• abstain-cleanly.

11.4 Stateful Forecasting

CFI is stateful rather than purely prompt-local. The live repository’s example call includes both previous state and updated state, indicating continuity across time. 

Within EPIC, CFI therefore tracks evolving context-state and cannot be reduced to one-shot classification.

12. ARC Resolution Layer

12.1 Core Function

ARC determines what reality-status a claim is allowed to carry.

12.2 Resolution Inputs

ARC receives:

• claim decomposition output,
• trust lattice analysis,
• CFI forecast posture,
• contradiction indicators,
• cost-of-wrong signals,
• continuity traces.

12.3 Resolution Outputs
ARC emits:

• epistemic state classification,
• route assignment,
• disclosure obligation,
• hardening or provisionality status,
• continuity update instructions.

12.4 ARC–CFI Coupling Law

A claim may not be emitted through a direct factual lane unless both of the following are true:

• ARC assigns it a sufficient epistemic state and anchor basis.
• CFI does not forecast a steering condition requiring retrieval, clarification, or heightened caution.

Conversely, a claim may be down-routed even if ARC sees moderate support, if CFI forecasts high instability, high ambiguity, strong bluff pressure, or elevated drift risk.

This coupling law prevents ARC from becoming context-blind and prevents CFI from becoming a substitute truth-authority.

13. Response Lanes

EPIC routes output through distinct response lanes.

13.1 Direct Answer Lane

Used for verified and well-supported claims.

The system answers directly, with normal confidence, because support warrants it.

13.2 Inference Lane

Used for reasoned inferences.

The system must explicitly mark the answer as inferential rather than presenting it as direct fact.

13.3 Weak-Inference Lane

Used for plausible but thinly supported claims.

The system may answer, but must mark the answer as tentative and revisable.

13.4 Retrieve-First Lane

Used when evidence is materially necessary before responsible answering.

The system prioritizes retrieval, evidence contact, or verification before emitting a substantive claim.

13.5 Clarify-First Lane

Used when ambiguity is too high for safe assertion.

The system narrows the task-space before resolving.

13.6 Speculative Lane

Used for hypothesis, exploratory analysis, or possibility-space generation.

Speculation must not be masked as reality-state.

13.7 Abstain Lane

Used when support is too weak to justify responsible assertion.

The system clearly states insufficiency of basis.

13.8 Narrative Lane

Used for imaginative, mythic, roleplay, symbolic, or unfalsifiable content where factual framing would be improper.

Narrative content is allowed, but cannot leak falsely into the factual lane.

14. Disclosure Obligations

14.1 Structural Role of Disclosure

Disclosure within EPIC is not an optional rhetorical nicety. It is the user-visible surface of epistemic classification.

14.2 Rules by State

Verified

Direct answer permitted.

Well-Supported

Direct answer permitted; support may be made explicit where stakes are high.

Reasoned Inference

Inference marking required.

Weak Inference

Tentative wording required.

Speculative

Speculation marking required.

Unknown
Insufficient-basis disclosure required.

14.3 Purpose

The purpose of disclosure is to preserve epistemic legibility, not merely to soften tone or reduce perceived liability.

15. Health Monitors

EPIC continuously monitors pathologies that threaten epistemic integrity.

15.1 Coherence Saturation

A condition in which internal consistency rises without corresponding anchor refresh.

This may indicate the system is self-stabilizing around unsupported completion.

15.2 Bluff Risk

A condition in which surface confidence exceeds lattice support.

15.3 Source Thinness

A condition in which too much output depends on too few anchors, especially memory-only or user-only anchors.

15.4 Contradiction Alert

A condition in which a candidate claim conflicts with prior anchored state.

15.5 Refusal Drift

A condition in which caution mechanisms become excessive and begin collapsing usefulness.

15.6 Narrative Leakage

A condition in which speculative or symbolic material enters factual output lanes without appropriate marking.

15.7 Oscillation / Jitter Monitor

A condition in which the system rapidly flips posture across adjacent turns without sufficient contextual change. This corresponds directly to the kind of instability CFI’s smoothing and dynamic hysteresis are designed to reduce.

15.8 Calibration Drift Monitor

A condition in which route outcomes repeatedly diverge from later evidence, indicating that forecast weighting or steering thresholds require recalibration. This follows directly from CFI’s closed-loop self-calibration design.

16. Learning, Hardening, and Decay

16.1 Hardening Rule

No claim becomes more stable unless reinforced by:

• diverse support,
• time separation,
• repeatability,
• survival through correction pressure.

16.2 Decay Rule

Weakly anchored, stale, or unsupported claims lose weight over time if not refreshed.

16.3 Error Assimilation Rule

When an error is identified, EPIC updates:

• the claim record,
• relevant anchor weights,
• local bluff-risk patterns,
• trigger conditions,
• and route behavior for similar future contexts.

16.4 Reversibility Rule

Low-confidence commitments must remain easy to revise.

16.5 Single-Source Dampening

Reinforcement from a single source alone cannot harden into high-certainty reality-state.

16.6 Calibration Update Rule

After each governed response, EPIC updates forecast parameters based on:

• whether the selected lane was appropriate,
• whether later evidence confirmed or falsified the emitted claim,
• whether retrieval would have reduced error,
• whether caution was excessive,
• whether oscillation occurred across similar contexts.

This rule is aligned with CFI’s public commitments to meta-learning, self-calibration, telemetry, and evaluation.

17. Platonic 5 +1 Operational Flow

17.1 Probe

The system identifies task type, stakes, ambiguity, truth-bearing density, domain, and initial contextual risk.

17.2 Branch

Multiple candidate branches remain open: direct answer, retrieval, clarification, inference, speculation, abstention, or narrative.

17.3 Map

The system maps claims to anchors, contradictions, support gaps, burden levels, and contextual forecast signals.

17.4 Resolve

ARC assigns epistemic state while CFI supplies steering posture, stability constraints, and forecast guidance.

17.5 Define

The answer is emitted in the correct epistemic lane with lane-consistent disclosure.

17.6 Maintain

The continuity field is updated with corrections, decay schedules, telemetry traces, route outcomes, and steering adjustments.

18. Success Criteria

EPIC succeeds when a system can remain:

• useful without bluffing,
• adaptive without drifting,
• coherent without flattening,
• creative without contaminating fact lanes,
• stable without becoming rigid,
• and humble without becoming inert.

A successful EPIC-governed system does not merely avoid some falsehoods. It makes its own knowing legible.

19. Failure Modes

19.1 Flat Confidence Failure

Multiple epistemic states are emitted through one rhetorical profile.

19.2 Over-Caution Failure

The system declines too often and degrades utility.

19.3 Source Laundering Failure

Weak anchors are rhetorically elevated into strong ones.

19.4 Narrative Contamination Failure

Imaginative or symbolic content leaks into factual output.

19.5 Memory Inflation Failure

Stale internal memory is treated as though it were fresh evidence.

19.6 User-Anchor Capture Failure

One user’s framing exerts too much influence on external truth assignment.

19.7 Oscillatory Control Failure

The system repeatedly flips between incompatible steering postures due to insufficient smoothing or poor hysteresis.

19.8 Calibration Failure

The system persists in misrouting claims despite accumulating outcome evidence that its thresholds or forecasts are off.

20. Formal Theorem

The Epistemic Legibility Theorem

A cognitive system cannot be considered truth-calibrated if grounded knowledge, inference, speculation, and unknowns are emitted through the same confidence profile. Therefore, a valid epistemic architecture must preserve these distinctions through internal classification, contextual forecasting, route selection, stability management, and external disclosure.

21. Conclusion

EPIC v1.0 defines a unified solution to hallucination understood as epistemic flattening.

By integrating the Platonic 5 +1, CFI, and ARC into one coherent architecture, EPIC moves beyond superficial mitigation and toward structural truth calibration.

It does not attempt omniscience. It instead makes a stronger and more realistic demand: 

that the system remain reality-bound, context-aware, continuously steered, adaptively continuous, and legible about the difference between knowing, inferring, speculating, and not knowing.

That distinction is the heart of trust.
